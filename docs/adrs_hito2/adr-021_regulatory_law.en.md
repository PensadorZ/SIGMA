# ADR-021: Regulatory and AI Risk Management Alignment — Multi-Jurisdictional Framework for SIGMA

## Executive Summary

This ADR replaces the previous version of `ADR-021`, which was limited
to SR 26-02. It broadens the scope to a full landscape of applicable
AI risk management frameworks and regulation: the **NIST AI RMF**,
**ISO/IEC 42001** (with explicit intent toward future certification),
**ISO/IEC 23894**, **U.S. state legislation** (case study: Colorado),
and **GDPR**. It also establishes a Base AI Management System (AIMS)
Program, viable for a sole operator, with a declared trajectory of
scaling toward formal certification as SIGMA grows. The SR 26-02 text
that motivated the first version is preserved in full in §5, as
historical background.

---

## Context

An agentic system that manages data — including text generated by
people via the sentiment analysis pipeline — operates in a fragmented
regulatory space, made up of voluntary frameworks with no legal force,
a certifiable standard with no immediate obligation to certify, and at
least one binding law that applies regardless of which artificial
intelligence (AI) framework an organization adopts.

The first of these frameworks is the **NIST AI Risk Management
Framework** (NIST AI RMF), published by the U.S. National Institute of
Standards and Technology (National Institute of Standards and
Technology [NIST], 2023). It is a voluntary, non-certifiable framework,
organized into four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage).

The second is the standard **ISO/IEC 42001:2023**, *Information
technology — Artificial intelligence — Management system*, jointly
published by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO)
and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) (International
Organization for Standardization & International Electrotechnical
Commission, 2023a). It establishes an AI Management System (AIMS) and,
unlike the NIST AI RMF, is certifiable through third-party external
audit.

The third is the standard **ISO/IEC 23894:2023**, *Information
technology — Artificial intelligence — Guidance on risk management*,
also from ISO/IEC (International Organization for Standardization &
International Electrotechnical Commission, 2023b). It is the
methodological guide for identifying, analyzing, evaluating, and
treating AI-specific risk, and extends the general ISO 31000 risk
management standard.

The fourth is the **General Data Protection Regulation** (GDPR),
Regulation (EU) 2016/679 of the European Parliament and of the Council
(European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2016). It is a
binding law with extraterritorial application: it governs the
processing of personal data of EU residents regardless of which AI
risk management framework an organization adopts.

The fifth is the **Colorado AI Act**, originally enacted as S.B. 24-205
(Colorado General Assembly, 2024) and substantially amended by
S.B. 189/S.B. 26-189, signed on May 14, 2026, which narrowed its scope
and postponed its effective date to January 1, 2027 (Colorado General
Assembly, 2026).

The sixth is the **SR 26-02** guidance (SR Letter 26-2), issued by the
Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System of the United States
(Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System, 2026), which
rescinds and replaces the SR 11-7 model risk management guidance.

From this point forward, the document uses only the acronyms (NIST AI
RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, ISO/IEC 23894, GDPR, Colorado AI Act, SR 26-02)
without repeating their expansion or full citation — the complete
bibliographic reference for each is consolidated in the References
section at the close of the document.

This ADR organizes that landscape and, in addition, formalizes the
commitment that SIGMA's governance does not wait until there is a team
or clients to be established — responsibility toward potential users
and clients begins with the sole operator, documented from the outset.

## Decision

### 2.1 — Framework landscape, kept distinct

| Framework | Nature | Jurisdiction | Applies to agentic AI? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NIST AI RMF 1.0** | Voluntary, non-certifiable, self-declarable | U.S. | Yes — technology-neutral, covers the full lifecycle |
| **ISO/IEC 23894** | Methodological risk management guidance (extends ISO 31000) | International | Yes — same scope as NIST AI RMF |
| **ISO/IEC 42001** | Certifiable management system standard (AIMS), audited by a third party | International | Yes — with a formal certification trajectory, see §2.3bis |
| **Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205, amended by SB 189, May 2026)** | Binding state law, effective date postponed to January 1, 2027 | Colorado, U.S. | Does not apply directly — the amended scope is limited to automated decision-making technology in employment, housing, credit, and healthcare; SIGMA does not make such decisions about people |
| **GDPR** | Binding law, extraterritorial application | European Union | Applies independently of any AI risk management framework, conditional on the actual processing of personal data — see §2.6 |

#### 2.3bis — Base AIMS Program: viable from the sole operator, with a scaling trajectory

Formal governance does not depend on team size. A Level 0 AIMS —
documented, with real evidence accumulating from the outset —
constitutes the legal and organizational foundation on which SIGMA
scales toward greater levels of responsibility as it incorporates real
users and clients.

**Level 0 — Sole operator, in progress:**

| AIMS element (reference ISO 42001 clause) | Current implementation |
|---|---|
| Documented AI policy (§5.2) | Formalized in `AGENTS_CREATOR.md`, as SIGMA's AIMS Policy |
| Declared scope of the management system (§4.3) | Explicit statement of components in scope (Director, Engineers, skills) and out of scope (external projects such as `ADR-B026`) |
| Roles and responsibilities (§5.3) | Documented by function — policy, review, operation — even when exercised by a single operator, following the same separation-of-duties principle that governs internal controls in small organizations |
| Risk register (§6.1) | The correspondence and gap tables in this ADR, maintained as a living record |
| Management review (§9.3) | Scheduled quarterly review of active ADRs, open gaps, and incidents from the period, documented with date and conclusions |
| Incident management (§8.2) | The `tracing.py` incident (Rollout 1, orphaned Langfuse observations), documented with root cause and fix, constitutes the first entry in the AIMS incident register |

**Level 1 — First real user or client data:**
Formal AI impact assessment prior to any deployment involving real
data; documented legal basis for any personal data processed; privacy
notice and terms of service before exposing SIGMA to a third party.

**Level 2 — Scaling to a team or formal clients:**
Internal audit (eventually by a contracted third party); consolidation
of the Level 0 and Level 1 evidence file; a decision, at that point, on
whether client volume or contractual requirements justify formal
ISO/IEC 42001 certification.

Each level constitutes accumulated, date-verifiable evidence, built as
a progressive governance foundation — not a promise deferred to an
indeterminate future.

### 2.5 — Colorado AI Act: why it does not apply today

The original law (2024) would have required a risk management program,
impact assessments, and a duty of care against algorithmic
discrimination for high-risk systems. The May 2026 amendment (SB 189)
removes those obligations and narrows the scope to disclosure and
transparency regarding automated decision-making technology in
employment, housing, credit, healthcare, and education, with the
effective date postponed to January 1, 2027. SIGMA does not make
consequential decisions about people in any of those categories — it
is a Big Data analysis tool, not an automated decision-making system
over subjects. This conclusion is not to be revisited unless SIGMA's
purpose changes toward decisions about people.

### 2.6 — GDPR: a parallel obligation, not resolved by any AI risk framework

No AI risk management framework replaces an already-applicable data
protection obligation. The development datasets used so far
(Tirendaz/Kaggle, Zenodo, Mendeley) are public and research-oriented —
a review of each dataset's description and terms was carried out,
confirming that they do not include declared sensitive information,
and Kaggle's usage policies explicitly prohibit the inclusion of
identifiable personal data by dataset publishers. This establishes a
reasonable basis, though not a formal legal audit, for holding that the
current development data does not contain personal data of EU
residents.

SIGMA, in any future deployment, could process data of identifiable
European subjects; in that scenario, the corresponding security
safeguards would apply (encryption in transit and at rest, data
minimization, documented legal basis prior to processing). This point
remains on standby, subject to further verification as EU and U.S.
legal provisions evolve.

### 2.7 — Relationship with `policies.yaml`

This ADR contains the reasoning, the per-framework mapping, and the
motivations. `policies.yaml` contains only the actionable runtime
parameters derived from this mapping — trust_level thresholds,
research-mode activation conditions, sandbox limits — with no
regulatory prose. This separation reaffirms the pattern already
established between `ADR-016`/`ADR-002`/`ADR-017` and `policies.yaml`.

### 2.8 — Language rule

Any material derived from this ADR, internal or external, must phrase
the alignment as: *"SIGMA applies, by design, AI risk management
principles aligned with the NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 23894, and
maintains a Base AI Management System Program oriented toward ISO/IEC
42001 certification, in a domain where binding regulation (SR 26-02,
Colorado AI Act) does not yet impose direct obligations."* Never "SIGMA
complies with [framework]" nor "SIGMA is certified" without qualifying
the actual level reached.

### 2.9 — Declared vision: SIGMA as a reference for agentic governance

SIGMA is expected, beyond aligning with existing frameworks, to
position itself in the medium term as a reference point for the
governance of agentic AI ecosystems. This ADR documents that direction
as the **declared motivation** for the Base AIMS Program in §2.3bis —
for when SIGMA occupies that place in the industry at some future
point in time. The distinction is not cosmetic: it is the legal
foundation that gives this document the support needed for users and
investors to have confidence, backed by evidence of progress from
Level 0 onward, and it is built with the approaching future in mind.
Responsibility toward future users and clients is precisely what
justifies starting the Base Program now, as a sole operator, rather
than waiting for the standard to be fully consolidated before managing
risk.

## Consequences

**Benefits:** scalability and certifiable, legally binding legitimacy
where applicable; establishment of the minimum requirements for a Base
AIMS Program with a clear progression toward certification; compliance
with applicable legal provisions so that users of this ecosystem can
feel secure.

**Risks and mitigations:**

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Being read as certification already obtained | Rule 2.8 explicitly distinguishes "oriented toward" from "certified" |
| Colorado's status changing before publication | Verification date documented in the frontmatter |
| GDPR being dismissed without full verification | §2.6 keeps it on standby, not as a closed conclusion |

## §5 — Historical Motivation: SR 26-02

> SR 26-02 rescinds and replaces SR 11-7, applicable to banking
> institutions with more than $30B in assets regulated by the Federal
> Reserve. It introduces a risk-based approach, proportional to model
> materiality, and formalizes *effective challenge*, *model inventory*,
> *ongoing monitoring*, and separation between development and
> validation.
>
> Note 3 of the document is explicit: *"Generative AI and agentic AI
> models are novel and rapidly evolving. As such, they are not within
> the scope of this guidance. Nonetheless, a banking organization's
> risk management and governance practices should guide the
> determination of appropriate governance and controls for any tools,
> processes, or systems not covered in this document."*

**Verified correspondences (preserved from the original):**

| SR 26-02 Principle | SIGMA Mechanism | Source ADR |
|---|---|---|
| *Effective challenge* | Mandatory human review of every ADR | `AGENTS_CREATOR.md` §2 |
| *Model Inventory* | Skill catalog + ADR table | `ADR-009` |
| *Documentation* | ADRs + Ag-DR | `ADR-018` |
| *Ongoing Model Monitoring* | Ag-DR, with a human approval gate | `ADR-018` §2.5 |
| *Outcomes Analysis* | `_run_permutation_bootstrap` in `0004-statistical-validator` | `ADR-001`, `ADR-009` |
| Development/validation separation | Engineer↔Engineer isolation | `ADR-016` §2.3 |

**Preserved historical gaps:** rigor proportional to materiality
(unresolved, a candidate for `0005-framework-selector`); third-party
vendor model validation (partially closed, `docs/model_cards/`, RoBERTa
commit hash still pending).
